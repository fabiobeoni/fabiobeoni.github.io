GETTING READY

WHAT SHOULD YOU DO TO PREPARE FOR THE WORKSHOP, AND HOW MUCH TIME DO YOU NEED?

Although training situations vary greatly and many factors may influence the preparation process (e.g. the level of experience of the facilitator, or the facilities and materials available), there are a number of steps involved in getting ready for this training:

1. Selection of the participants

We recommend a minimum of 10 and a maximum of 30 participants. Consider these selection criteria:
  • Select participants with a similar level of knowledge of the Voluntary Guidelines.
  • Select participants from all the three sectors (i.e. land, fishery and forest).
  • Consider gender balance among the participants to allow both men and women to benefit from the training.

2. The training team

Set up a training team well in advance of the training. Ideally, the team should be involved throughout the process (e.g. preparatory steps, implementation of the training, and monitoring and evaluation activities following the training). Here are some of the key tasks of the training team:
  • Review together this learning guide prior to the training, to gain a full and common understanding of the programme.
  • Gather knowledge and skills related to the governance of tenure (e.g. tenure issues in country, legal negotiation, tenure reform processes) and on facilitation.
  • Define in advance the roles and responsibilities for each session.
  • Make a list of what has to be prepared in advance, the materials needed, and the timing.
What about support staff? Support staff should be available full time for at least three days before the training begins, as they will play a critical role in preparing the training materials, photocopying documents, and helping the facilitator(s) during the training. In addition, a staff member or consultant should take care of documentation, take notes and photos during the training, type up outputs as needed during the training, and prepare the training report.

3. Background reading

A key step for the training team is to review the following background materials:
  • Voluntary guidelines on the responsible governance of tenure of land, fisheries and forests in the context of national food security.
  • E- learning module: Introduction to the responsible governance of tenure.
  • People’s Manual.

4. Conducting a national context analysis

The analysis of the national context is critical, as it will give participants an overview of the main issues related to the governance of tenure in the country. The analysis may be facilitated either by the team or by a consultant, who should go through the background reading (see above) prior to analysing the national context. The outputs of the analysis will inform: 
  • Which aspects of the national context should be presented to participants in Session 15 (National Context Analysis). 
  • How to amend sessions 8, 9, 10, 11 and 15.
  • Who are the actors involved in tenure governance (inputs for sessions 12 and 14).

5. Choosing a training venue

  1. Size and location: The geographic location of the venue as well as the actual physical space such as the size and layout of the room can significantly influence the outcome of the training. The training venue should have a large hall with lots of space for the participants to move around. Ideally, the capacity of the room should be approximately double the number of participants. It would be important to have places where 3 or 4 smaller groups of roughly 5–6 people each can meet separately for group work. Alternatively, more than one room might be needed.
  2. Room layout: Chairs should be easily movable and arranged in a ‘U’ shape with no tables in front of chairs, with only 3 or 4 tables behind the U against the walls, or alternatively with small tables for 6 to 8 people for the working sessions. There should be a small table for the LCD projector. The facilitator and the training team should ideally sit with participants in the ‘U’, or at one small table close to the participants. Should this not be possible, the facilitator should avoid sitting behind a big table at the front. 
  3. Wall space: Ideally, there should be large white boards or chalk boards. If boards are not available, thereshould be lots of wall space for placing things on the wall

6. Preparing training materials and equipment

  1. Adapt and prepare the materials: Review the materials related to the sessions, adapt them to the training context, and decide which materials should be distributed as hard copies to the participants. A translation into the local language might be needed when the participants do not master the language of the materials. Make sure there are enough copies of materials for each participant, and ensure that materials are photocopied, stapled and put where they can be easily retrieved for the appropriate session. Preferably, do not give out all materials at once.
  2. Buy materials following a checklist: It is important to buy materials carefully and in advance, especially if the venue is a long way from shops. A materials checklist is provided below. This should be adapted to local names for materials, printed out and used by the person doing the purchasing. Because the training involves group exercises using cards and big sheets of paper, a lot of materials may be needed, so it is always good to overestimate if the number of participants is uncertain.
  3. Allocate time and location for preparing materials. Many big posters have to be prepared before the training. You should prepare them all before the training starts. Refer to the Training materials checklist to prepare in advance. 

Tips and comments

1. The training organizers should begin preparations approximately 1–1.5 months prior to the training. Consider that a full understanding of this learning guide and of the proposed methodology may require some additional time. 

Other key factors that may have an impact on the time needed include:

  • The level of familiarity of the facilitator with the Voluntary Guidelines, the tenure situation of the country, and the tenure-related processes at national and local levels.
  • The need to adapt the training material to the local context, including to translate the training material into local language(s).

Carry out a careful assessment of these elements before starting the preparation, to plan realistically for the work that needs to be done in preparation for the training.

2. Allow one day for rehearsal: talking through and practising each session brings the guide to life and helps the team to think through the process and ensure that all the materials are in place. 

3. Remember to provide participants with a copy of the VGGT during the training. In addition, all participants should either bring or be supplied with notebooks and pens to use throughout the workshop.
